Abandoned Places In California

Discover California's Abandoned locaitons

Name: Bodie Methodist Church

Location: Bodie State Park, CA

Cordinates: 38.21238247983349, -119.01420190002423

Status: Active

Bodie State Park is situated in the North-East of California, close to Mono Lake, and stands at an elevation of about 8,375 feet. It was once a thriving gold rush town in 1876, with a population of 5,000-7,000 people and millions of dollars of gold being mined locally. Despite this, the boom was short-lived, and the town's population began to decline in 1880 as miners moved elsewhere. The town was designated a National Historic Landmark in 1961, and Bodie State Historic Park was established in 1962 to maintain the town's state of "arrested decay." Although it's located about 12 miles from the main road, and the last three miles are a dirt track, it remains an intriguing place to visit (McBey, 2020) [1].

Name: Mount Umunhum's Radio Tower

Location: San Jose, CA

Cordinates: 31.670580, -106.009912

Status: Active

Mt. Umunhum, a mountain in California, is easily identifiable by its unique shape, which is due to an abandoned radio tower known as the Mt. Umunhum Cube. Built during the Cold War era in the early 1960s, the five-story building was used for sky surveillance and enemy aircraft tracking. However, it was left to decay after the airbase that ran it was decommissioned in 1980. Despite being off-limits for safety reasons, the Mt. Umunhum Cube remains a popular destination and landmark for visitors, although all doors and windows are boarded up. The Midpeninsula Regional Open Space District has reopened the summit to the public after years of closure, and visitors can now drive to the top and park near the Cube, as well as explore new trails and a ceremonial space that honors the site's Native American history (akornblatt, 2016) [2].

Name: The Bayshore Roundhouse

Location: Brisbane, CA

Cordinates: 37.701844, -122.406912

Status: Active

The Bayshore Roundhouse was once a bustling railroad hub, housing not only a huge freight yard and a large number of inbound and outbound tracks but also an on-site hospital for its 3,000 employees (Garr, 2018). Despite being built between 1907 and 1910 by the Southern Pacific Railroad, the Bayshore Roundhouse became obsolete by 1958, leading to its eventual abandonment in 1982 (Garr, 2018). The decay started soon after its closure and was further exacerbated by a fire in 2001, which destroyed one side of the roundhouse, leaving only the Flat Side standing in ruins today (Garr, 2018). Although the site is now fenced off due to safety concerns, it remains an intriguing reminder of the area's industrial past [3].
